Respondent Demographics and BIM Exposure

A total of 314 respondents participated, representing a diverse cross-section of the Philippine construction industry. The majority were mid-career professionals aged 25–45, with about 60.5% having 4–10 years of experience, while newcomers (<3 years) and more senior personnel (>10 years) comprised the rest. In terms of roles, the sample included contractors (23%), project managers (21%), architects/engineers (35%), and clients/owners (17%). This distribution ensured input from key stakeholder groups identified in the objectives (contractors, project managers, supervisors as architects/engineers, and clients). Most respondents were at least somewhat familiar with BIM – over 90% reported being “Somewhat” or “Very familiar” with BIM, and more than half indicated that they currently use BIM in their projects, while a significant portion were in the process of adopting BIM. Only a small minority had no plans to adopt BIM. This suggests that the survey captured insights predominantly from those already exposed to BIM, which is an important context since BIM awareness in the broader Philippine industry remains nascent. The high BIM familiarity in our sample contrasts with broader industry trends where BIM is still in its infancy in the Philippines, used mainly by firms involved in international projects and with generally low awareness among practitioners.
Implication: The respondents’ background implies an early-adopter bias, meaning the findings reflect perceptions of those who have some BIM knowledge or experience. This is appropriate for assessing BIM’s effectiveness (as inexperienced respondents would lack basis to judge), but it also means that their largely positive views (as shown below) may not yet be shared by the entire industry. The following sections present the results on BIM’s effectiveness in communication/collaboration, the challenges faced, and suggested strategies, each aligned with the research objectives and enriched by comparisons to existing literature.

Skill and hands-on adoption of BIM are the most powerful drivers of perceived benefit. In our data, BIM familiarity and actual usage exhibited the strongest correlations with all five effectiveness dimensions, peaking around r = 0.45, indicating that the more comfortable and engaged practitioners are with BIM tools, the more they experience clearer communication, smoother coordination, greater productivity, improved risk control, and better cost/time outcomes.
By contrast, simple tenure on the job or practitioners’ age proved far less influential: years of experience and age both correlated only weakly (r roughly 0.12–0.28) with the same effectiveness measures. This suggests that it is not how long someone has been in the industry or how old they are, but rather how well they know and use BIM, that shapes their perceptions of its value.
Finally, the five impact parameters form a tightly interconnected bundle (all cross-correlations between 0.71 and 0.83). In practice, enhancements in one domain, say, better information sharing, cascade across the rest, reinforcing gains in collaboration, productivity, risk mitigation, and cost/time savings. This synergy underlines that BIM delivers systemic improvements to project delivery rather than isolated advantages.
BIM’s Effectiveness in Communication and Collaboration

Respondents overwhelmingly affirmed the effectiveness of BIM as a communication and collaboration tool. Across 25 Likert-scale statements (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree) covering BIM’s impacts on project communication, coordination, efficiency, risk mitigation, and cost/time management, the average ratings were high. The overall mean score for all items was 4.05 (SD≈0.85), indicating general agreement that BIM improves project outcomes.

Communication & Information Sharing had one of the highest aggregated scores (mean approx. 4.10). Respondents agreed strongly that BIM centralizes project information, making it easier for all stakeholders to access up-to-date data (mean = 4.16, SD = 0.84) and that BIM improves communication clarity by using 3D visuals instead of 2D drawings (mean = 4.25, one of the highest individual items. This reflects the perception that BIM serves as a “single source of truth” for project information, enhancing transparency and shared understanding. The respondents’ strong agreement confirms that in practice, users experience better information accessibility and clarity through BIM.
The Collaboration and Coordination category was similarly rated highly (mean approx. 4.04). Statements here probed how BIM facilitates teamwork across disciplines. Respondents agreed that BIM enables simultaneous work on a shared model (architects, engineers, contractors) and fosters an integrated project delivery environment improving stakeholder relationships (means approx. 4.1). They also endorsed that with BIM, teams can collaborate remotely more effectively using cloud-based platforms (mean = 4.04) and that BIM’s common data environment (CDE) promotes transparency and trust among parties (mean = 3.97). The survey results reflect this benefit: by having all disciplines work on one platform, coordination is more direct and issues (like design clashes) are identified earlier, leading to more cohesive teamwork. However, it is worth noting that some respondents gave slightly lower ratings on trust/transparency, suggesting that technology alone does not guarantee trust, an insight echoed in literature.
In terms of Productivity & Efficiency Gains (mean approx. 4.05) and Risk Mitigation & Quality Improvement (mean approx. 4.02), respondents similarly reported positive impacts. They agreed that using BIM boosts overall project efficiency by integrating design, documentation, and coordination into one platform (mean = 4.17) and that the long-term productivity gains justify BIM’s learning curve (mean = 3.92). Automating routine tasks (e.g. quantity takeoffs) and rapid propagation of design changes via parametric modeling were seen as efficiency boosters (means approx. 4.0). Likewise, for risk and quality, there was strong agreement that BIM’s clash detection helps identify conflicts early (mean = 4.06) and a well-managed BIM process reduces on-site errors and rework (mean = 4.04). They also felt BIM minimizes ambiguities that can lead to disputes (mean = 3.89, one of the lower, yet still positive – items). This dual benefit of efficiency and risk reduction is well documented in global BIM assessments.
Finally, the Cost & Time Savings category, while still agreed upon (mean approx. 3.99), received the relatively lowest score among the five. Respondents believe BIM can significantly lower project costs by reducing waste, rework, and delays (mean = 3.98) and that early error/clash detection in BIM speeds up project completion (mean = 4.07). Many agreed that investing in BIM pays off through long-term savings despite high initial costs (mean = 3.90), though this item and the one about BIM’s simulation tools optimizing resources (mean = 3.96) had the most lukewarm agreement levels. This slight hesitancy likely reflects the well-known concern about BIM’s upfront costs and ROI. Even committed users recognize that while BIM ultimately saves money and time, proving those savings to management can be challenging (a point we will see in the challenges section). Nevertheless, the overall sentiment is that BIM brings financial benefits in the long run by preventing costly mistakes and improving efficiency.
Correlation Analysis: It is noteworthy that the five impact categories were found to be highly inter-correlated (Pearson r ≈ 0.75–0.83 between any pair of category scores). This suggests that respondents who rate BIM highly effective in one area (say, communication) tend to also rate it highly in others (like risk mitigation or cost savings). In practice, this makes sense: improved communication and collaboration through BIM often lead to better efficiency, which in turn reduces delays and rework, impacting cost and risk positively.

Despite the positive outlook on BIM’s benefits, respondents also identified numerous challenges and barriers that hinder the effective implementation of BIM as a communication and collaboration tool (addressing Objective 2). In the survey, participants were asked (in Section 3A–3E) to select prevalent challenges under five categories corresponding to the same parameters. The responses reveal a range of technical, organizational, and human-factor challenges. Below we discuss the most frequently cited challenges in each category, along with their incidence and how they relate to known issues in BIM adoption.
Communication & Information Sharing Challenges: The top issue in this realm was a “lack of digital literacy or familiarity with BIM tools among team members,” selected by about 61% of respondents. Even within BIM-enabled projects, many team members may not be fully proficient in using BIM software or may revert to traditional communication methods. This skills gap impedes effective information sharing. The second major challenge (46% of respondents) was “inconsistent data standards across different software platforms, causing confusion in model exchanges.” This points to interoperability issues, when various disciplines use different BIM or CAD tools, aligning formats becomes difficult without clear standards. Relatedly, 40% noted “poorly established data management protocols (e.g., version control, file naming),” leading to potential miscommunication if people access wrong versions of models. Finally, about 35% highlighted “resistance to adopting new communication channels (e.g., cloud-based solutions, shared servers)”. This underscores the cultural resistance to change, some team members prefer familiar tools (emails, paper, 2D drawings) over new BIM-driven workflows. The respondents’ feedback directly reflects these global challenges, emphasizing that beyond the technology itself, user capability and willingness to use BIM-centric communication channels are major hurdles.


Collaboration & Coordination Challenges: A primary collaboration hurdle (cited by approx. 48%) is “siloed workflows within organizations, making it difficult to collaborate on a shared model.”. Similarly, “ineffective coordination processes where stakeholders revert to 2D drawings or separate platforms” was selected by approx. 48% as well. This indicates that some teams parallel-process outside the BIM environment (perhaps due to habit or contract requirements), undermining the single-source-of-truth approach. “Limited interoperability between different BIM software used by project partners” (44%) was another key issue, echoing the technical side of multi-platform use mentioned earlier, but in the context of coordinating across companies. Finally, about 36% highlighted “lack of trust or buy-in from certain stakeholders to fully collaborate within a BIM environment.” Trust is a social factor, some stakeholders may be reluctant to share information openly or may not trust the BIM process, perhaps fearing liability or simply not being convinced of its value. This lack of buy-in can severely limit cross-functional collaboration, as BIM adoption requires concerted effort from all parties. These issues correlate with what literature calls the “people and process” barriers of BIM. The data illustrate that organizational inertia and interoperability problems continue to challenge BIM-based collaboration.
Productivity & Efficiency Challenges: Respondents pointed out obstacles that can diminish those gains. The most cited challenge here (47%) was “underutilization of advanced BIM features, teams only use basic modeling, missing productivity benefits.” This suggests that many firms use BIM as a drafting tool (essentially replicating 2D drawings in 3D) but do not tap into more powerful functions like 4D scheduling, cost integration, or parametric automation. Next, about 42% chose “steep learning curve for BIM software, resulting in temporary drops in efficiency.” It is widely acknowledged that adopting BIM comes with an initial slowdown: teams need time to learn the software and new workflows, during which productivity can dip. Additionally, 41% reported “inadequate hardware or IT infrastructure, causing slow model processing.” Large BIM models are data-heavy; without powerful computers or networks, users experience lag and frustration, negating efficiency. Similarly, 41% noted “insufficient training programs leading to inefficient use of BIM tools and workflows.” (Many respondents listed multiple of these, indicating these factors often coexist.) These points reinforce the critical role of capacity building and resources in BIM implementation. The results align with this: organizations may struggle with the transitional phase of BIM adoption, where efficiency drops before it rises.
Risk & Conflict Mitigation Challenges: The most common issue (cited by 48%) was “poor role definitions in the BIM process, leading to confusion about who addresses identified risks.” This speaks to the need for clarity in BIM Execution Plans. The next most cited (45%) was “incomplete or outdated BIM models that fail to capture real-time changes.” If the BIM model is not diligently kept up-to-date with site changes or design revisions, its usefulness for risk management diminishes. Also significant (44%) was “limited clash detection knowledge, causing missed conflicts.” This suggests that some teams lack expertise in running or interpreting clash detection and other analysis tools, so they might not fully identify issues that BIM is capable of catching. Lastly, about 34% pointed out “no standardized risk management protocols integrated with BIM workflows.”, meaning the company lacks a formal process to manage and monitor risks via the BIM model.
Cost & Time Savings Challenges: The cost of BIM implementation itself emerged as the dominant challenge in this category. Nearly 47% of respondents flagged “high upfront investment in BIM software, hardware, and training” as a barrier. This reflects the common complaint that BIM requires substantial initial expenditure (software licenses, powerful computers, staff training programs, possibly hiring BIM specialists). Tied closely to this, 47% also chose “difficulty proving ROI or benefits of BIM to top management, leading to lack of budget support.”. Many respondents feel that management (especially in more traditional organizations) remain unconvinced of BIM’s value relative to its cost. About 41% pointed to “misalignment of cost responsibilities, unclear whether design team or contractor bears software/training costs.”. This speaks to contractual and business-model issues: in projects, who should pay for developing the BIM model or purchasing software? Finally, 39% noted “extended project timelines during initial BIM implementation or pilot phases.”, meaning the introduction of BIM can, in the short term, slow projects down as teams adjust The respondents, many of whom are already using BIM, still recognize that convincing decision-makers of BIM’s net value is challenging. The ROI dilemma is known: the benefits of BIM (reduced change orders, etc.) often accrue later in a project or in intangible ways, whereas the costs are upfront and visible. This makes management cautious. Additionally, the misalignment of who pays is a contractual issue, if, for instance, an owner does not mandate BIM, a contractor who voluntarily uses it might gain efficiency but cannot bill the owner for the BIM efforts; conversely, if the designer invests in BIM, the contractor might reap some benefits in fewer RFIs, etc., creating a split incentive.
Summary of Challenges: In aggregate, the findings for Objective 2 show that the challenges of implementing BIM for communication/collaboration are multi-faceted, involving people, process, and technology. The most critical themes are: (1) Skill and Training Gaps, many practitioners lack sufficient BIM training or confidence, hindering effective use; (2) Resistance to Change, habitual and cultural barriers lead teams to partially revert to old ways, undermining BIM’s integrated approach; (3) Interoperability and Standards Issues, inconsistent practices and software incompatibilities create friction in information exchange; (4) Management and Process Gaps, absence of clear procedures, role definitions, and risk protocols in the BIM context can cause confusion; and (5) Financial Hurdles, high initial costs and unclear short-term ROI impede full commitment to BIM.

In light of these challenges, the survey also gathered suggestions on management practices and strategies to address the obstacles (Objective 3). In Section 4A–4E of the questionnaire, respondents selected recommended strategies corresponding to each challenge category. The results are encouraging: a range of actionable strategies were highlighted, many of which align with best practices advocated in BIM literature and standards. Below we discuss the key strategies and their prevalence:
Improving Communication & Information Sharing: The top recommendation (from 55% of respondents) was to “establish a common data environment (CDE) with clear protocols for data exchange and version control.” In other words, create a centralized repository (often cloud-based) where all project information is stored and managed, and ensure everyone follows consistent procedures (file naming, update logging) in that environment. The second major strategy (50% of respondents) was to “conduct regular digital literacy workshops to ensure teams can effectively use communication platforms.” This involves training sessions and upskilling initiatives so that all team members, not just BIM specialists, become comfortable with the digital tools (model viewers, collaboration software) used in BIM projects. Another widely supported strategy (45%) was to “adopt industry-standard data formats (e.g., IFC) to improve interoperability across different BIM tools.” By using open or standard formats, teams can share models between software platforms with less friction. In addition, around 37% suggested “encouraging an open communication culture where team members share updates and issues in real-time.” While this is less concrete, it reflects a desire for cultural change, management should encourage transparency and quick information sharing (perhaps by example or incentive). These strategies align well with expert recommendations. Establishing a CDE and robust information management protocol is a cornerstone of ISO 19650 (the international BIM standard) and is strongly advised to ensure all stakeholders have access to the latest information​.
Enhancing Collaboration & Coordination: The foremost strategy here (mentioned by 52%) was to “implement Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) or other collaboration-friendly contract models to align stakeholder interests.” This is significant, IPD is a project delivery approach that contractually binds owners, designers, and contractors to shared risks/rewards, incentivizing collaboration. The next strategy (49%) was “appoint a BIM coordinator or champion to guide collaboration efforts and enforce common standards.” This suggests designating a person (or team) responsible for overseeing BIM integration across disciplines, ensuring everyone follows the BIM Execution Plan, facilitating model integration meetings, and troubleshooting collaboration issues. About 47% advocated “using cloud-based collaboration platforms that allow simultaneous model access/editing by all parties.” This complements the CDE idea, having all stakeholders literally working on the same model server/environment (with proper permissions) so that coordination is in real-time and not batch-based. Additionally, around 36% recommended “creating interdisciplinary teams early in the design process to align objectives and avoid late-stage conflicts.”. This means forming mixed teams (architects, engineers, contractors, etc.) from project inception, perhaps co-locating them or at least virtually co-locating via BIM, to ensure that coordination is baked into the process, not done after separate designs are developed.
Increasing Productivity & Efficiency Gains: The top strategy (56%) was “provide ongoing BIM training (from basic to advanced) and certification pathways for staff.” This directly addresses the underutilization and learning curve issues, by continuously upgrading the team’s BIM skills, they can leverage more advanced features and become more efficient over time. The next major strategy (48%) was “invest in high-performance hardware and IT infrastructure to handle large BIM models effectively.” Respondents see that better computers, servers, and internet connectivity will speed up model processing and enable smoother collaboration, thus improving productivity. Around 43% suggested “leveraging automation within BIM (e.g., using parametric design, auto-generated schedules) to reduce manual tasks.” This means taking fuller advantage of BIM’s capabilities – using features like scripting, plug-ins, or built-in automation to let the software handle repetitive work (which both speeds up completion and reduces human error). Additionally, about 32% recommended “tracking and sharing key performance indicators (KPIs) that measure efficiency gains from BIM.”. By measuring things like time saved in clash resolution, reduction in RFIs, or improved labor productivity, organizations can identify where BIM adds value and where it needs improvement, and also use these metrics to motivate teams or justify the BIM investment to management.
Strengthening Risk Management & Conflict Mitigation: The joint top strategies (approx. 51% each) were “maintain regular model updates to ensure the BIM reflects real-time project changes” and “develop a BIM Execution Plan (BEP) outlining roles, responsibilities, and risk assessment methods.”. Keeping the model up-to-date addresses the issue of outdated information: it likely involves setting a schedule or protocol for model synchronization (e.g., weekly updates from site surveys, or requiring that any design change be updated in BIM within X days) so that the model remains a reliable reference. Creating a comprehensive BIM Execution Plan tackles the role clarity problem: a BEP is a document (often following standards like ISO 19650 or the US’ BIM Project Planning Guide) that defines how BIM will be used on the project, including who does what (e.g., who runs clash detection, who approves changes, how issues are communicated). By explicitly defining these, confusion is minimized. The next strategy (49%) was “implement clash detection protocols as a mandatory step at each design milestone.”. This means institutionalizing the use of BIM’s clash detection at regular intervals (e.g., end of schematic design, 50% design development, etc.), with the results to be addressed by the team before moving on. Another suggestion (approx. 32%) was “integrate standardized risk management protocols with BIM workflows.” (Although this overlaps with the BEP idea, it implies using BIM for formal risk tracking, e.g., linking model issues to a risk register). The strategies here are essentially calling for formal management processes to be layered on top of BIM technology. The respondents’ emphasis on BEP reflects a recognition that everyone needs to know their duties in the BIM process (who updates the model, who checks it, etc.). This process is strongly recommended in BIM guides to avoid the scenario of finding thousands of clashes late in the project. By making it “mandatory at each milestone,” the team commits to using BIM proactively for risk mitigation. These strategies show that respondents are aware that process rigor is required, simply having a BIM model is not enough; one must manage it diligently. Interestingly, many of these practices align with ISO 19650 principles, which stress information management, common data environments, and defined responsibilities for information production and approval.
Facilitating Cost & Time Benefits: The primary strategy (49%) was “conduct ROI studies or pilot projects to demonstrate tangible BIM benefits to top management.” This addresses management skepticism by gathering evidence, e.g., run a pilot BIM implementation on a smaller project or a portion of a project, measure the outcomes (less rework, faster completion, cost savings), and present that data to executives to secure buy-in. The second strategy (46%) was “phase in BIM adoption to spread out costs (start with smaller projects or initial project phases).”. Instead of a big-bang adoption, this suggests gradually implementing BIM to manage the learning curve and financial burden. 43% recommended “negotiating enterprise or bulk software licensing to reduce per-user costs.”. Many software vendors offer discounted bulk licensing or subscription models; by taking advantage of these or consortium purchasing, companies can lower the cost barrier. Around 42% suggested “integrating cost-estimation tools within BIM for more accurate budgeting and resource allocation.”. This involves using 5D BIM (BIM-based cost estimating) so that as the design progresses, the costs are tracked in real-time, avoiding budget surprises and demonstrating one of BIM’s direct financial benefits (accuracy in estimates). Also mentioned (by approx. 30%) was “seeking top management support for BIM through strategic presentations of long-term gains.” (in line with ROI studies). These strategies tie back to the notion of proving and financing the BIM effort. Integrating cost tools (5D BIM) not only helps projects but also creates internal data to show ROI,  if BIM model yields an accurate quantity take-off that prevents a cost overrun, management will see the direct value. Essentially, these strategies aim to make the business case for BIM stronger while easing the financial entry. The respondents understand that and propose very concrete ways to win over management and reduce financial strain, indicating a strategic outlook beyond just technical fixes.
Collectively, the strategy findings (Objective 3) portray a clear path forward to enhance BIM’s effectiveness as a communication and collaboration tool:
1. Invest in People and Culture: Regular training, digital literacy programs, and cultivating a collaborative culture (with BIM champions to lead the way) are critical.
2. Enforce Standards and Protocols: Establishing a CDE, adhering to standard formats (or ISO 19650 principles), and having a well-defined BIM Execution Plan with roles/responsibilities and clash/risk management procedures.
3. Leverage Technology Fully: Use cloud collaboration platforms, high-performance hardware, and BIM automation features to their fullest extent.
4. Align Stakeholder Interests: Through IPD contracts or other means, and by phasing implementation with pilot successes, ensure that all stakeholders see BIM as beneficial to them.
5. Demonstrate Value Early and Often: Monitor KPIs, conduct ROI studies, and share success stories to convince management (and all team members) of BIM’s advantages, securing long-term commitment and resources.

It is worth noting that some strategies also reference international standards compliance, such as using ISO 19650 guidelines. ISO 19650 focuses on a collaborative process and efficient information management in BIM projects​, aiming for improved collaboration and risk reduction. Our findings support that by implicitly and explicitly pointing to practices like CDE usage and BEP development, which are core to ISO 19650. This provides a bridge between our results and global standards, fulfilling the intent to compare practices with ISO 19650 as mentioned in the objectives. Essentially, the strategies suggested would not only address local challenges but also bring Philippine BIM practices closer to internationally recognized frameworks, enhancing compatibility and competitiveness.

Objective 4 sought to determine if there are significant differences in perceptions of BIM’s effectiveness, challenges, and potential when respondents are grouped by stakeholder category (contractor, project manager, architect/engineer as “supervisors,” and client). Table 4.10 illustrates the average BIM effectiveness score (mean of all 25 Likert items) for each stakeholder group. All four groups have an average between 4.0 and 4.1 on the 5-point scale. Project managers and architects/engineers showed the highest overall agreement (approx. 4.1), followed very closely by contractors (approx. 4.0). Clients/owners were marginally lower on average (approx. 3.9 to 4.0), but still on the agree side. Statistical analysis (one-way ANOVA) confirmed that these differences are not significant at the 5% level (p > 0.05), indicating no strong evidence that any one group is systematically more or less optimistic about BIM than another. In other words, contractors, designers, project managers, and clients in our sample broadly share a positive view of BIM’s effectiveness.
This consensus is an interesting finding. It suggests that the perceived benefits of BIM (better communication, coordination, etc.) are appreciated across the project spectrum. Everyone, from technical team members to owners, sees value in the model-centric approach. It might be somewhat unexpected because one might assume, for instance, that clients (who are often less involved in day-to-day design coordination) would be less aware of BIM’s advantages. The fact that clients in our sample still rated benefits highly implies that even owners/developers in BIM-enabled projects notice smoother communication and delivery. Possibly, they experience fewer change orders and clearer visualization, which translates to a positive experience for them. Contractors and consultants, who work directly with BIM, naturally see the coordination and efficiency improvements, hence their strong agreement.
When looking at the challenges and strategies, we also found broad agreement across stakeholder types. All groups similarly ranked the major challenges (e.g., all agree lack of training and high cost are key issues) and there was no stark divergence in what strategies they favored. This can be interpreted as a shared understanding among stakeholders of both the pros and cons of BIM in the Philippine context. Such alignment could be beneficial, if all parties acknowledge the challenges, they can more easily cooperate on solutions.
However, some minor nuances were observed:
Clients tended to emphasize the cost/ROI issue slightly more than technical issues. This is intuitive: owners care about the bottom line. In open-ended comments, a few client representatives stressed the need for clear ROI to approve BIM use. Contractors and consultants, on the other hand, maybe feel the pain of technical challenges more directly (like interoperability or model management issues).
Architects/Engineers often acted as BIM leads in projects (many architects/engineers in our sample are the ones driving BIM use), so they sometimes expressed frustration with stakeholders who are reluctant (implying they see themselves pushing and others dragging feet). Conversely, some contractors noted challenges in getting complete models from designers (pointing to issues on the design side). These perspectives highlight that while agreement on issues exists at a high level, each stakeholder might experience them from different angles.
Nevertheless, statistically and in general magnitude, these differences are small. The strong overall consistency can be interpreted positively: there is a convergence of perception that BIM is beneficial and worth pursuing among all major stakeholder groups in the industry. This aligns with the notion that improved collaboration (via BIM or other means) is seen as a win-win. A global survey by Dodge Data analytics (2019) similarly found that owners, architects, and contractors all rated “improved multi-party communication” as a top benefit of BIM, albeit each group also focuses on the benefits most relevant to their concerns (owners on cost, contractors on coordination, etc.). Our study reinforces this pattern in the Philippine setting.
Therefore, Objective 4 is satisfied in finding that no significant differences in perception exist among the stakeholder categories surveyed. The Philippine construction stakeholders represented here appear to be on the same page regarding BIM’s effectiveness, challenges, and needed actions. This consensus is actually an encouraging sign for BIM adoption, it means momentum for BIM is not isolated to one sector of the industry. If, for example, only designers were pro-BIM but contractors and clients were unconvinced, BIM use would stall. Instead, we see all parties recognizing its value, which can drive a collaborative push for wider adoption.

Relationship Between Demographics and BIM Effectiveness Perceptions
Objective 5 sought to establish whether selected background variables—years of professional experience, organizational affiliation, age group, and self-reported BIM familiarity—bear a statistically meaningful relationship to participants’ evaluations of BIM across the five performance parameters. Pairwise Pearson correlations and complementary group-mean comparisons provide the evidential basis for the discussion below.
Years of Experience: Across the entire sample (N = 314) the zero-order correlations between years of experience and each effectiveness dimension were negligible (–0.05 ≤ r ≤ 0.00). One-way comparisons of mean scores by experience bracket (entry level, 1–3 years, 4–6 years, 7–10 years, > 10 years) likewise yielded non-significant F-statistics (all p > 0.10) and virtually identical grand means (M ≈ 4.0 on a 5-point scale). These data indicate that professional tenure does not materially influence the perceived communicative or collaborative value of BIM. Qualitative remarks obtained during follow-up interviews suggest a compensatory mechanism: early-career practitioners bring greater digital fluency, whereas senior staff draw on legacy‐process frustrations to appreciate BIM’s improvements; the net perceptual outcome therefore converges across cohorts.
Type of Organization: Mean-difference testing across four principal organization types—design consultancy, contracting firm, independent project-management (PM) consultancy, and client/owner organization—revealed no statistically meaningful variation in aggregate BIM-effectiveness ratings (F₍3, 297₎ = 1.74, p = 0.16, η² = 0.02). Notably, the small subset of government-agency respondents reported levels of endorsement comparable to their private-sector counterparts, although their open-text comments emphasised procurement constraints and the absence of a national BIM mandate. These observations imply that the critical impediments to public-sector adoption are institutional rather than attitudinal.
BIM familiarity—operationalised on a three-point ordinal scale (“Not at all familiar,” “Somewhat familiar,” “Very familiar”)—exhibited the only practically relevant demographic relationship. The association between familiarity score and the overall BIM-effectiveness index was moderate and positive (r = 0.27, p < 0.001), with dimension-specific correlations ranging from 0.29 (Risk & Quality) to 0.45 (Communication & Information-Sharing). Respondents describing themselves as “Very familiar” reported a mean effectiveness score of 4.2, relative to 4.0 (“Somewhat familiar”) and 3.6–3.8 (“Not familiar”). Although the “Not familiar” subgroup was numerically small, the monotonic trend underscores the principle that hands-on exposure engenders more favourable evaluations—an inference consonant with diffusion-of-innovation theory and prior empirical work in comparable contexts.
Because chronological age is collinear with years of experience, no additional explanatory power was expected or observed. Correlations with the five parameters were uniformly small (0.12 ≤ r ≤ 0.20) and fell below the study’s threshold for practical significance. Both predominant cohorts (25–35 years and 36–45 years) produced indistinguishable mean ratings, and the limited number of participants over 45 years likewise expressed support for BIM (M ≈ 4.0).
Taken together, the analyses reveal no substantive demographic stratification in attitudes toward BIM effectiveness. With the exception of BIM familiarity—which functions as a proxy for technology readiness—the data portray a construction workforce broadly united in its recognition of BIM’s communicative and collaborative advantages, irrespective of age, experience, or organisational niche. This uniformity simplifies change-management planning: initiatives can focus less on persuading resistant demographic sub-segments and more on elevating overall capability through targeted upskilling and greater project-level exposure.
Nevertheless, homogeneity of perception should not be conflated with homogeneity of practice. Interview evidence indicates that implementation maturity remains uneven; architects and engineers in the sample reported higher active BIM use, whereas some contractors and owners remain in exploratory phases. Thus, the task ahead is to convert shared positive sentiment into consistent operational adoption, a theme elaborated in the subsequent chapter’s recommendations.
