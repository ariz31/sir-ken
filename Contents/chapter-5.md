Chapter Five
SUMMARY, CONCLUSIONS, AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Summary of Findings
This study set out to assess the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry, identify key challenges in its implementation, determine strategies to address these challenges (in line with ISO 19650 where applicable), examine differences in perceptions among stakeholder groups, and explore relationships between demographics and perceived BIM effectiveness. Through a survey of 314 construction professionals and subsequent data analysis, the following key findings were obtained:
BIM’s Effectiveness (Objective 1): BIM was found to enhance communication and collaboration significantly in construction projects. Respondents overwhelmingly agreed that BIM leads to better information sharing, improved multi-disciplinary coordination, increased productivity/efficiency, enhanced risk mitigation/quality control, and cost/time savings. Average agreement ratings were around 4 out of 5 across five evaluated dimensions (Project Efficiency, Stakeholder Engagement/Collaboration, Decision-Making, Cost/Resource Optimization, Risk Mitigation & Quality Improvement). Particularly, BIM’s ability to serve as a single source of truth (via centralized 3D models and common data environments) was highlighted, resulting in clearer communication, fewer misunderstandings, and more timely decisions. These results affirm that, in practice, BIM functions as intended: a platform that integrates project information and stakeholders, leading to smoother project delivery​. The high scores across all parameters indicate that practitioners are experiencing meaningful improvements in project outcomes when using BIM, directly addressing the long-standing communication and fragmentation issues in construction. The findings thus confirm that BIM is effective in meeting the communication/collaboration performance metrics identified in earlier chapters (such as improving accuracy, timeliness, clarity of information, and stakeholder engagement).
Challenges in BIM Implementation (Objective 2): Despite recognizing BIM’s benefits, respondents identified numerous challenges and barriers preventing full realization of those benefits. The most critical challenges include: (a) Lack of BIM skills and training, many team members are not adequately trained to use BIM tools, leading to underutilization and errors; (b) Resistance to change, habitual reliance on traditional methods (2D drawings, siloed workflows) and reluctance to adopt new processes or technologies; (c) Interoperability issues and lack of standards, difficulties in exchanging data between different software, and absence of unified data management protocols, causing confusion; (d) Unclear processes and responsibilities, without clear BIM execution plans, teams face confusion over roles (e.g., who updates the model, who fixes clashes), and important tasks (like model updating or clash resolution) may fall through the cracks; (e) High initial costs and ROI concerns, the investment in software, hardware, and training is high, and many companies struggle to justify these costs due to uncertain short-term returns or lack of client demand. These challenges mirror global BIM adoption issues​ and reflect the Philippine industry’s current state: while some projects use BIM, they do so in a less-than-optimal environment where organizational readiness and external support (e.g., standards, mandates) are still developing. Consequently, BIM’s effectiveness can be blunted by these barriers – for instance, if models are not updated or people don’t use them properly, the communication benefits diminish. The study systematically documented these pain points, providing empirical backing to the qualitative challenges discussed in Chapter 2 (literature review). No fundamentally new barriers emerged beyond those known internationally, suggesting that the Philippines faces the same issues of technology, process, and people, only potentially more accentuated by local industry conditions such as limited training opportunities and lack of enforced standards.
Strategies and Management Practices (Objective 3): The study identified a set of strategies to improve BIM adoption and efficacy, which the respondents believe will address the aforementioned challenges. Key strategies include: (a) Training and capacity building, implementing regular BIM training programs, workshops, and even certifications to raise the skill level across all project participants; (b) Establishing a Common Data Environment (CDE) and standards, using a centralized platform for all project data with strict version control and adopting standards (like ISO 19650 guidelines, or at least consistent naming conventions and data formats) to ensure interoperability and clarity​; (c) BIM Execution Planning, developing comprehensive BIM Execution Plans at project outset that define roles, responsibilities, processes (for clash detection, updates, approvals, etc.), aligned with best practices and possibly ISO 19650 Part 2 requirements for information management​; (d) Leadership and collaboration frameworks, appointing BIM champions/coordinators to lead implementation and foster a collaborative culture, and utilizing collaboration-friendly contract models like Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) to align stakeholder incentives and reduce resistance​; (e) Phased implementation and demonstrating value, starting with pilot projects or phased rollouts to manage costs and learning curves, and actively measuring and communicating BIM’s benefits (through KPIs or ROI studies) to gain top management and client buy-in; (f) Investing in infrastructure and automation, upgrading hardware/IT infrastructure to support BIM and employing advanced BIM features (parametric design, 4D/5D, automation scripts) to maximize productivity gains. These strategies directly counter the identified challenges: for example, training tackles skill gaps, the CDE/standards address data exchange issues, IPD and champions target the cultural resistance, and phased ROI-focused approaches mitigate financial concerns. The high level of agreement on these strategies among respondents suggests a readiness to embrace these improvements. Moreover, these strategies align closely with global recommendations and ISO 19650 principles, indicating that stakeholders are aiming to elevate local BIM practices to international standards of information management and collaboration​. In essence, the study not only pinpointed problems but also mapped out solutions that industry practitioners believe in – painting a roadmap for organizations and the industry at large to follow.
Stakeholder Group Perspectives (Objective 4): The analysis revealed that different stakeholder groups (contractors, project managers, architects/engineers, clients) largely share the same positive perception of BIM’s effectiveness and acknowledge similar challenges and needed strategies. No statistically significant differences were found in their responses. All groups rated BIM’s impact highly (mean scores approx. 4). This consensus implies a cross-cutting recognition of BIM’s value. Minor nuances were noted (e.g., clients emphasizing ROI a bit more, designers frustrated with lagging collaborators), but these do not amount to fundamental disagreements. The lack of divergence is an important finding: it indicates that efforts to promote BIM do not face an uphill battle with any particular sector resisting the idea of BIM. In fact, contractors and owners, who might traditionally be seen as less involved in design technology, appear just as appreciative of BIM’s benefits as architects and engineers. This uniformity could be a product of the sampling (those who responded are inherently more BIM-aware), but it is nonetheless a promising sign of industry alignment. It suggests that if challenges are addressed, all stakeholders are likely to cooperate in BIM implementation rather than some dragging their feet. The study thereby concludes that any perception differences by stakeholder role are minimal, and that the drive for better communication/collaboration through BIM is a shared interest.
Demographic Factors (Objective 5): The study did not find strong correlations between demographic variables (like years of experience, age, organizational type) and perceptions of BIM effectiveness. Both younger and older professionals, and those from different types of companies, showed broadly similar attitudes. One notable point was BIM familiarity: individuals very familiar with BIM were naturally a bit more positive, while those not familiar were more cautious. This underscores the importance of exposure, direct experience with BIM tends to reinforce positive views, likely because people witness its advantages first-hand. Aside from that, the general lack of demographic disparity implies that the need for BIM and the challenges in BIM are not confined to a subset of the industry; they are felt across the board. Everyone, regardless of background, benefits when BIM is implemented well and suffers when it is not. This reinforces the idea that BIM is not a niche concern but a broad industry innovation. It also means that improvement initiatives (training, standards, etc.) should be rolled out widely, not just targeting, say, younger staff or specific firms, the whole industry workforce can be uplifted together.
In summary, the study’s findings present a coherent narrative: BIM is widely seen as a powerful tool to improve project communication and collaboration in the Philippines, but its current implementation faces obstacles that are well-understood and for which clear remedies are proposed. The perceptions are consistent across stakeholder groups and demographics, indicating a unified front ready to move forward if given the opportunity and support. These results validate many points raised in the literature review (Chapter 2), for example, confirming that issues like poor collaboration and miscommunication in projects can be mitigated by BIM​, that the Philippines’ slow BIM uptake is due to factors like cost and lack of expertise, and that strategies such as training, setting up CDEs, and adopting IPD could significantly improve outcomes​. By aligning the empirical evidence with the research objectives, the study provides a strong basis for conclusions and recommendations geared toward harnessing BIM’s full potential in the local construction industry.
Conclusions
Based on the above findings, several conclusions can be drawn regarding BIM’s effectiveness as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry and the broader implications for project management practices:
BIM significantly improves project communication and stakeholder collaboration
 The study conclusively found that BIM, when used, addresses many chronic communication challenges in construction projects. It enables faster and clearer information flow, ensures that all stakeholders are on the same page by using the same up-to-date model, and reduces fragmentation of information. Stakeholders experience fewer instances of miscommunication (such as errors from using outdated drawings or misunderstandings of complex details) because BIM’s visual and data-rich environment enhances clarity and transparency​. Furthermore, BIM fosters a more collaborative atmosphere: architects, engineers, contractors, and owners can interact with the model, identify issues together (e.g., clash detection meetings), and make collective decisions, which builds a shared understanding and trust. This aligns with the notion that BIM is not just a technology but a process enabler, it provides a platform for Integrated Project Delivery in spirit, if not contractually, by integrating the contributions of various parties. As a result, projects using BIM tend to have better-coordinated outcomes, as evidenced by respondents’ strong agreement on improvements in efficiency, decision-making, and risk reduction. We conclude that the effective use of BIM leads to enhanced project performance: communication is more timely (real-time updates), information is more accurate (single source reduces errors), decisions are made with better data, and overall collaboration is improved, which can translate into tangible benefits like reduced rework, fewer delays, and smoother project execution. This validates the theoretical expectations set out in Chapter 1, BIM can be a potent solution to communication and coordination problems that have long plagued the construction industry.


The potential of BIM is not fully realized due to implementation barriers While BIM’s efficacy is proven in concept and in isolated success stories, the Philippine industry has yet to unlock its full value across the board. The study shows that many projects suffer from partial or suboptimal BIM adoption: e.g., a project may use BIM for design coordination but not keep the model updated during construction, limiting its usefulness; or a company may purchase BIM software but not invest in training, leading to poor usage. These gaps are primarily caused by the identified challenges: insufficient training leads to reliance on old practices; lack of standards and protocols causes confusion and inconsistent use of BIM; cultural resistance means some team members or even entire firms opt out of BIM participation; and high costs without clear immediate returns discourage companies from embracing BIM wholeheartedly. Thus, a key conclusion is that the effectiveness of BIM in improving communication/collaboration is currently dampened by these barriers. Until they are addressed, BIM’s impact will remain fragmented, pockets of excellence in a few projects or companies, rather than industry-wide gains. The Philippine construction industry finds itself at a juncture where most stakeholders believe in BIM’s benefits (as our data shows), but structural impediments keep many from acting on those beliefs fully. This situation is consistent with the observation that the country’s BIM adoption is in an early stage: interest is high, but maturity is low. Therefore, to truly leverage BIM for better project communication and collaboration on a national scale, these implementation challenges must be systematically tackled. If not, BIM’s promise will remain underfulfilled, and traditional inefficiencies will persist despite the availability of modern technology.

There is strong alignment between stakeholders on the need for change and the strategies to achieve it
One heartening conclusion from this research is that the various parties in construction – owners, contractors, consultants – are not at odds about BIM. All see its value and, importantly, all recognize what needs to be done to improve BIM adoption: training, better coordination mechanisms, management support, etc. This collective awareness is an asset. It implies that initiatives to improve BIM implementation will likely find willing collaborators across the project team spectrum. For example, if an owner mandates the use of a CDE and provides support for training, contractors and designers are unlikely to push back since they themselves identified those as beneficial. The consistency of perspectives revealed by the study suggests that the industry is coalescing around a common vision of a BIM-enabled future, even if progress has been slow. This also means that solutions can be holistic – one need not create separate plans for each stakeholder type; a general improvement program (such as a joint training workshop or a project-wide BIM execution plan meeting) will resonate with all. The conclusions drawn here reinforce that improving communication and collaboration is a shared goal, and BIM is seen by all as a key means to that end. Thus, efforts like developing Philippine BIM standards or offering multi-stakeholder BIM training sessions are likely to be met with enthusiasm rather than resistance.
Management commitment and strategic implementation are critical to overcoming the current challenges
The study’s findings on challenges and strategies lead to an overarching conclusion: the difference between mediocre and excellent BIM outcomes often comes down to management practices and strategic choices, not just the technology itself. BIM requires a conducive environment to flourish. If top management in a firm (or a project) is committed to BIM, they will invest in training, enforce collaboration protocols, insist on updated models, and be patient in expecting ROI, thereby likely achieving the benefits in time. Conversely, if management is lukewarm, BIM use might be haphazard, training ignored, and old habits allowed to continue, resulting in suboptimal results that then reinforce management’s skepticism, a self-fulfilling prophecy of failure. This study provides evidence that many of the barriers are surmountable with proper strategy: for instance, the high cost can be mitigated by phased implementation and clearly demonstrating ROI​; interoperability issues can be solved by agreeing on open standards; skill gaps can be filled by ongoing education. What it takes is leadership and planning, someone to champion these strategies and allocate resources to them. Thus, a crucial conclusion is that the effectiveness of BIM as a collaboration tool is not just a technical issue but a management one. Organizations that treat BIM adoption as a strategic change (with change management processes, clear goals, and measurement) tend to succeed, whereas those that treat it casually (just buying software) tend to struggle. In the Philippine context, this might extend beyond individual companies to industry bodies and government: leadership at those levels (e.g., formulating national BIM guidelines, offering incentives) could play a decisive role in surmounting challenges that individual projects alone cannot (like lack of standardization or unwillingness of some stakeholders).

Alignment with international standards and practices will accelerate BIM benefits
The study found that many strategies suggested (CDE, BEP, IPD, etc.) align with ISO 19650 and global best practices. A conclusion drawn from this is that the closer the Philippine industry moves to these established frameworks, the more it can reap BIM’s benefits effectively and uniformly. ISO 19650 provides a ready-made blueprint for managing information in BIM projects (including communication protocols and roles), which directly addresses multiple challenges our respondents noted. If companies and projects start adopting ISO 19650 (as some pioneering firms in the Philippines have begun to​, they will likely find that issues of data consistency, role ambiguity, and interoperability are reduced, because the standard enforces a consistent approach and fosters collaboration and transparency​. Likewise, learning from international experience, for instance, how Singapore or the UK implemented BIM mandates, could help avoid pitfalls. The solutions our respondents gravitated towards indicate an understanding that we don’t need to reinvent the wheel; the industry can leapfrog by learning from global BIM leaders. Therefore, a key conclusion is that adopting international best practices (tailored appropriately to local context) is a strategic way to overcome local BIM implementation challenges and ensure that BIM’s role in improving communication/collaboration is maximized. This also positions the Philippine construction industry to be more globally competitive and collaborative on international projects, as it will be using the same “language” of BIM standards as other countries.

In summary, the conclusions of this study are optimistic yet pragmatic: BIM works and is a game-changer for project communication and teamwork, but unlocking its full potential in the Philippines requires concerted effort to break down current barriers. The industry stakeholders understand both the value and the work needed to attain that value. With unified vision and strong leadership, BIM can substantially elevate the efficiency and effectiveness of Philippine construction projects, aligning them with modern, collaborative practices seen globally​. The study thus reinforces the central thesis that BIM, complemented by appropriate management strategies, can transform the construction process from a fragmented one to an integrated one, addressing many of the root causes of project delays, cost overruns, and quality issues.
Recommendations
Drawing from the findings and conclusions, this section provides recommendations for various levels of stakeholders, individual firms, project teams, industry organizations, and policymakers – to enhance the adoption and effective use of BIM as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry. The recommendations are aimed at systematically addressing the identified challenges and leveraging the suggested strategies, thereby operationalizing the improvements needed.
1. Invest in Comprehensive BIM Training and Capacity Development: Lack of user proficiency is a fundamental barrier; hence, all organizations involved in construction (design firms, contractors, project management consultants, even client organizations with in-house project teams) should invest in regular and structured BIM training programs. This includes basic training for new users (to cover BIM software operation and collaborative workflows) and advanced training for experienced users (to cover topics like BIM uses for simulation, 4D/5D, and coordination management). It is recommended to establish a “BIM competency development roadmap” within organizations, whereby staff progress through levels of BIM skill certification. Firms could collaborate with educational institutions or professional associations to run certified courses or workshops. Moreover, cross-training across disciplines should be encouraged, e.g., site engineers learning model navigation to extract information, or architects learning how contractors use the model for planning – to promote empathy and better communication. Industry associations (like the Philippine Institute of Civil Engineers, United Architects of the Philippines, etc.) should also facilitate frequent seminars, webinars, and case study presentations on BIM best practices to broaden awareness. By upskilling the workforce, the industry ensures that technology is used as intended. This addresses the root cause of many downstream issues and ensures teams can fully utilize BIM’s features to communicate and collaborate. In the long term, it’s advisable to incorporate BIM in university curricula for engineering and architecture (as an outcome of collaboration between academia and industry) so that new graduates enter the field with at least a basic BIM literacy, reducing the training burden on companies. As literature notes, when BIM is woven into education, professionals are better prepared to exploit it for communication and coordination from the get-go​.
2. Develop and Implement Project-Specific BIM Execution Plans (BEP) and Standards: For each project that adopts BIM, stakeholders should collaboratively develop a detailed BIM Execution Plan at the project initiation stage. The BEP should outline: project BIM goals (e.g., “eliminate 90% of major clashes before construction” or “achieve paper-less coordination meetings”), roles and responsibilities of each party in the BIM process (who authors models, who federates them, who runs clash detection, who approves changes, aligning with the roles defined in ISO 19650, such as Project Information Manager, Task Team Manager, etc.), the BIM deliverables and milestones, processes for model updates and issue resolution, standards for modeling (level of development detail, naming conventions), and the use of a Common Data Environment. All key stakeholders (design consultants, contractor, subcontractors, client’s representative) should sign on to this BEP, indicating their commitment. Templates for BEPs are available (e.g., from the US National BIM Standard or international examples); these can be adapted to the Philippine context. Additionally, teams should agree on standardized classification and data exchange formats, for example, using IFC (Industry Foundation Classes) as an export/import format when different software are used, and following a consistent classification system for model elements (like MasterFormat or UniFormat) to ease communication. Adopting such standards will reduce confusion and errors in information sharing. By having a robust BEP and data standards in place, projects will have a clear roadmap for collaboration, reducing ambiguity and preventing many of the coordination breakdowns that occur when BIM is used ad-hoc. Essentially, this recommendation operationalizes the strategy of enforcing protocols and ensures accountability. It is in line with ISO 19650 Part 2’s recommendation to have “information management assignments and a plan”​. We further recommend that owners or project initiators require a BEP as part of project kickoff (possibly even as part of bid submissions, contractors and designers can be asked to propose a draft BEP), as this will institutionalize the practice. Case studies from other countries show that projects with a well-defined BEP have more successful BIM outcomes.
3. Establish a Common Data Environment (CDE) for Projects: Hand-in-hand with the BEP, every BIM-using project should establish a Common Data Environment, which is a centralized repository of all project information, accessible to authorized stakeholders. The CDE could be a cloud-based platform (there are many, such as Autodesk BIM 360, Bentley ProjectWise, Trimble Connect, etc.) or a server solution that all parties agree to use. All project documents, models, and data should be stored in the CDE, and all exchanges of information should happen through it. Within the CDE, define workflow states (e.g., work-in-progress, shared, published, archived) as per ISO 19650 guidelines for how information moves from individual authors to the wider team​. Implement strict version control: each model update or document upload should be logged with metadata (date, author, changes made) so that there is full transparency. Also, permissions should be set such that everyone can access what they need, but sensitive changes are controlled. A well-managed CDE will ensure that every team member is accessing the latest information at all times, greatly reducing version confusion and “I didn’t get that file” scenarios. It also facilitates asynchronous collaboration, where teams in different locations or time zones can contribute and review on their own schedule but within the same environment. We recommend that companies invest in the necessary CDE platform and include in the BEP training for all participants on how to use it effectively (e.g., how to check out a model, how to comment or mark up issues, etc.). Over time, using a CDE will become habit, and it will build trust as everyone sees that the system is transparent. This directly tackles multiple issues: it addresses data inconsistency, encourages real-time updates, and promotes a culture of sharing. Global experience has shown that a CDE is a backbone of successful collaborative BIM projects and this should be no different in the Philippines.
4. Implement Phased BIM Adoption with Pilot Projects and Monitor Outcomes: For organizations or sectors that are still hesitant about BIM (perhaps due to cost or uncertainty), a phased approach is recommended. Start with a pilot project or a pilot phase within a project. Choose a project of manageable size (or a particular part of a larger project) and implement BIM workflows there first. Before the pilot, define what success looks like, e.g., reduce RFIs by X% compared to a similar non-BIM project, or save Y amount of time in coordination, and then measure it. Ensure the pilot is properly resourced (training, time, etc.) to give it a fair chance. After completion, analyze the outcomes and document the benefits (and lessons learned). Use these documented results to build the business case (ROI) for broader BIM implementation. Essentially, generate local evidence of BIM’s value. The recommendation is that company executives and project decision-makers who are on the fence should support at least one pilot to see results for themselves. Government agencies (DPWH, for instance) could also do this by mandating BIM on a small public project as a trial, gathering data from it, and then refining requirements for larger projects. During phased adoption, prioritize implementing the most beneficial or easiest-to-win aspects first, for example, many start with 3D coordination (clash detection) as the first BIM use, since it directly reduces rework, then gradually add 4D scheduling or cost estimation in later projects once the team is comfortable. This incremental build-up prevents overwhelming teams and spreads out the investment over time​. Alongside, develop metrics: track the number of clashes detected vs. clashes found on-site, track communication response times, track change order costs, etc., to tangibly see improvements. The data from our study suggests stakeholders are keen on seeing proof of ROI, so organizations should institutionalize performance tracking for BIM. When benefits can be demonstrated in concrete terms (as 91% of contractors/owners in one study agreed, better collaboration through BIM reduces project risk), it will create a virtuous cycle: management becomes more willing to invest, which improves implementation, which yields more benefits. We recommend publishing or sharing pilot project outcomes within the industry (conferences or case study papers) to spread knowledge and build overall confidence in BIM.
In conclusion, these four recommendations form a multi-tiered approach that mirrors the key pillars of “people, processes, technology, incentives, and institutionalization.” By investing in comprehensive training (people), developing robust Execution Plans and standards (processes), establishing a Common Data Environment (technology), piloting BIM with clear ROI metrics (incentives), and embedding best practices into organizational routines (institutionalization), the industry creates a self-reinforcing system in which BIM can truly thrive. Implemented together, they will enable more Philippine projects to leverage BIM’s communication and collaboration strengths, translating into fewer delays and cost overruns, higher build quality, and more cohesive project teams. Over time, this integrated approach will elevate national productivity and competitiveness, moving the sector away from fragmented, paper-based methods toward modern, data-driven workflows. While the journey demands commitment and investment, our findings show that stakeholders are ready and willing to take it on. By following these recommendations, the industry will fully realize the mantra that underpins this research: Building Information Modeling, when backed by the right skills, structured processes, and collaborative frameworks—is a powerful unifier that aligns all project participants around common goals and drives superior project outcomes.












APPENDIX A:
ISO 19650 Checklist
1. Project Information Management
·   	Have the Project Information Requirements (PIR) been clearly defined?
·   	Are Asset Information Requirements (AIR) established to align with operational needs?
·   	Are the requirements documented and accessible to all stakeholders?
2. Common Data Environment (CDE)
·   	Is a centralized CDE in place for managing and sharing project information?
·   	Are protocols for information upload, retrieval, and revision control implemented?
·   	Is the CDE aligned with security and accessibility guidelines?
3. Roles and Responsibilities
·   	Are the roles of the Appointing Party, Lead Appointed Party, and Task Teams clearly defined?
·   	Are responsibilities for information management distributed and understood by all stakeholders?
4. Information Delivery Plans
·   	Has a Master Information Delivery Plan (MIDP) been developed to outline deliverables and timelines?
·   	Are Task Information Delivery Plans (TIDPs) established for each team?
5. Collaboration and Communication
·   	Are collaboration and communication protocols standardized across teams?
·   	Are mechanisms in place to resolve conflicts or issues in a timely manner?
6. Information Exchange
·   	Are standardized formats for information exchange defined and adhered to?
·   	Are information exchanges tracked to ensure accuracy and timeliness?
7. Data Quality and Security
·   	Are data quality checks performed regularly to ensure consistency and reliability?
·   	Is an information security protocol implemented to protect sensitive project data?
8. Lifecycle Integration
·   	Is information management integrated across all lifecycle phases of the project?
·   	Are lessons learned from previous phases documented and applied?
9. Continuous Improvement
·   	Are there regular reviews of information management processes?
·   	Is stakeholder feedback incorporated to enhance workflows?
10. Sustainability Considerations
·   	Are the information management processes aligned with sustainable practices?
·   	Are resource use and waste reduction considered in decision-making?
