Chapter One
THE PROBLEM AND ITS SETTING
A. Background of the Study

Globally, the construction industry played a foundational role in economic development, delivering critical infrastructure for transportation, housing, utilities, and public services. Despite its contributions, the industry consistently faced persistent challenges in communication and collaboration, particularly in large-scale, multidisciplinary projects involving architects, engineers, project managers, contractors, and subcontractors. These professionals, often working remotely or across different phases of a project, relied heavily on effective communication to ensure a shared understanding of project objectives, design intent, task responsibilities, and safety protocols. However, miscommunication, ambiguity, and delays remained common, especially in projects with complex scopes and layered organizational structures.
During the course of many construction projects, communication practices had been fragmented. Project stakeholders often depended on a mix of phone calls, text messages, emails, messaging apps, and informal meetings to exchange updates and resolve issues. Although these channels enabled quick interaction, their lack of integration frequently led to inconsistent information flows, misaligned expectations, and errors in execution. These problems contributed to rework, missed deadlines, and cost escalations. The absence of centralized documentation or real-time coordination systems further worsened the situation, as project drawings, schedules, and updates were sometimes outdated, inaccessible, or misunderstood.
Another critical issue observed in the industry was early-stage misalignment. Project teams often lacked a unified understanding of deliverables, milestones, responsibilities, and constraints at the onset. This lack of alignment led to inefficient task allocation, unrealistic timeframes, unclear contact points, and scope misinterpretations, eventually resulting in resource misallocation and project disruption. While some level of scope evolution was expected in construction, poor coordination at the start significantly increased the risk of misunderstandings and performance gaps.
Additionally, many projects experienced difficulties in managing essential data such as architectural drawings, schedules, permits, and regulatory requirements. Information was scattered across disconnected systems and platforms, and stakeholders were frequently overwhelmed with nonessential or redundant data, creating an environment of information overload. These inefficiencies resulted in poor decision-making, outdated guidance, and difficulties in ensuring compliance or responding to changes in real time. Organizations that addressed these issues began implementing centralized document control systems and structured onboarding programs, allowing teams to tie documentation to specific workflows and minimize confusion.
It also became evident that decision-making processes were affected by slow approvals and a lack of real-time project data. The absence of timely and reliable information limited stakeholders’ ability to make informed decisions, particularly in high-pressure or rapidly evolving project environments. As a result, collaboration suffered, and progress stalled, despite the best efforts of project leaders and technical personnel.
To address these longstanding problems, the construction industry turned to digital tools, including Building Information Modeling (BIM), social media platforms, and Information and Communication Technology (ICT) systems. Of these, BIM emerged as one of the most transformative solutions. BIM enabled teams to develop centralized digital models containing real-time data about a project’s physical and functional characteristics. These models helped visualize the project across various stages, from design to delivery, and improved coordination among teams. Research by Eastman et al. (2020) underscored how 3D and 4D BIM models allowed stakeholders to detect design conflicts early and correct them before construction began, thereby avoiding rework and delays.
BIM’s benefits also included enhanced accuracy and timeliness of shared information, as noted by Kwofie, Aigbavboa, and Baiden-Amissah (2020). However, their findings also revealed limitations such as information overload, restricted data access (gatekeeping), and lack of role clarity. These limitations made it difficult for all project members to access the information they needed or fully understand their responsibilities. Thus, even as BIM was adopted for its visualization and coordination advantages, it could not resolve communication issues on its own unless supported by sound management practices.
Other tools such as social media were also used to enhance communication in construction projects. Platforms like WhatsApp and Facebook Messenger were leveraged for real-time updates, instant feedback, and quick coordination. Research by Yang, Ahuja, and Shankar (2019) confirmed that social media could promote timely feedback loops and improve team cohesion. Nonetheless, they also warned of associated risks like information overload, data privacy breaches, and the lack of professionalism, which sometimes led to communication inefficiencies rather than improvements.
To measure the quality of project communication more objectively, Trach, Lesovik, and Tomaszkiewicz (2023) applied fuzzy logic and Social Network Analysis (SNA) to evaluate stakeholder interactions. Fuzzy logic translated subjective judgments, such as clarity, timeliness, and accuracy, into measurable data. SNA further identified critical communication roles (e.g., project managers, site engineers) and highlighted the risks of overcentralized information flows. Their findings suggested that when too much information depended on a few individuals, the project’s efficiency became vulnerable to bottlenecks or personnel turnover.
Regionally, studies from Poland and other European countries emphasized that communication challenges were among the leading causes of project delays and cost overruns. Kania, Radziszewska-Zielina, and Śladowski (2021) recommended adopting digital tools like BIM to streamline communication flows, particularly in project environments involving multiple, rotating team members. These findings aligned with the realities observed in the Philippine construction sector, which faced similar systemic communication issues.
In the Philippines, the construction industry experienced parallel challenges. Although recognized as a major economic driver, the sector remained dependent on manual workflows and traditional communication methods. While some large-scale projects had adopted BIM for coordination and visualization, its broader implementation remained limited. According to a national study by Bagcal et al. (2019), only 33.6% of professionals in the AEC industry were actively using BIM despite the industry encompassing over 16,300 licensed contractors. High software costs, lack of skilled personnel, resistance to change, and unclear data governance policies were cited as major barriers.
To bridge these challenges, international standards like ISO 19650 provided a roadmap for organizing project information through BIM. ISO 19650 emphasized structured data exchange, role clarity, accountability, and the use of a Common Data Environment (CDE) to improve transparency. The standard also encouraged efficient information workflows through the development of Master Information Delivery Plans (MIDPs) and Task Information Delivery Plans (TIDPs). When applied effectively, these frameworks helped eliminate data fragmentation and enabled better stakeholder collaboration.
This study, therefore, investigated the effectiveness of BIM in improving communication and collaboration within the Philippine construction industry. Specifically, it assessed BIM's impact on five key parameters:
1. Project Efficiency - Since BIM’s integrated modeling has been shown to cut project timelines and reduce errors. For instance, Das, Khursheed, and Paul (2025) reported that BIM adoption across multiple case studies reduced project durations by an average of 20 % and costs by 15 %, while decreasing design errors by 30 % and RFIs by 25 %.
2. Stakeholder Engagement – It centralized 3D/4D visualizations foster greater participation and shared understanding. The National Institute of Building Sciences (2021) found that BIM implementations increased stakeholder involvement in review sessions by over 40 %, improved asset quality, and reduced rework and waste through transparent, model‑based workflows.
3. Decision‑Making Processes – It emphasized BIM’s real‑time data capabilities, which streamline approval cycles. RICS (2024) highlighted that BIM‑driven dashboards, fed by live sensor and progress data, accelerated decision‑making and improved accuracy in change management by providing instant access to up‑to‑date project information.
4. Cost and Resource Optimization – It shows the importance in controlling budgets and minimizing waste. Mohamed et al. (2024) demonstrated that integrating 5D BIM with Value Engineering yielded a 35 % reduction in overall material costs and a 42 % cut in floor material usage on a residential twin‑villa case study, thereby optimizing resource allocation and budget adherence.
5. Risk Mitigation and Quality Improvement - BIM’s clash detection and risk‑analysis tools enhance safety and quality control. Moshtaghian and Noorzai (2023) applied an AHP‑based BIM risk management framework and showed that it prioritized financial, sustainability, and safety risks dynamically, leading to more proactive hazard identification and a measurable improvement in overall project performance.
It also explored the challenges that hindered BIM implementation, identified management strategies that addressed those barriers, and evaluated how these strategies aligned with ISO 19650 standards. Furthermore, the study examined how stakeholder perceptions varied across roles (contractors, project managers, architects/engineers, and clients) and how demographic factors such as professional experience and organization type influenced those perceptions.
Ultimately, the study aimed to generate evidence-based insights and practical recommendations to help the Philippine construction industry transition more effectively into a collaborative, digital future. By addressing persistent communication issues and leveraging BIM's full potential through structured frameworks like ISO 19650, the industry stood to benefit from improved project delivery, greater accountability, and enhanced sustainability.
B. Statement of the Problem

Despite the global shift toward digital technologies in construction, the Philippine sector continued to face persistent challenges in communication and collaboration. These issues significantly affected project timelines, quality, cost efficiency, and stakeholder coordination. The complexity of projects involving multidisciplinary teams operating under tight schedules further exacerbated these problems.
Traditional communication methods—such as phone calls, emails, text messages, and informal meetings—were widely used. While they enabled quick exchanges, their fragmented use often led to information gaps, delayed feedback, and unclear responsibilities. As a result, many projects suffered from rework, cost overruns, and inefficiencies.
Building Information Modeling (BIM) emerged as a potential solution, offering a centralized digital platform for integrating and managing project data. It enabled teams to visualize tasks, manage information, and collaborate in real time. However, BIM adoption in the Philippines remained uneven, especially among small to medium-sized firms, due to technical, financial, and organizational barriers that limited its full impact.
Even among adopters, questions remained about BIM’s effectiveness in improving communication. It was unclear how well BIM supported key performance parameters such as project efficiency, stakeholder engagement, decision-making, cost and resource optimization, and risk mitigation. Evidence was also limited on the barriers to full BIM integration and how local practices aligned with international standards like ISO 19650.
Moreover, it was uncertain whether stakeholder perceptions of BIM’s effectiveness differed across roles—such as contractors, project managers, architects, engineers, and clients. Each group had different communication needs and responsibilities that could shape their experience. Likewise, factors such as professional role, years of experience, and organization type may have influenced how BIM was perceived as a communication and collaboration tool.

C. Significance of the Study
This study aims to evaluate the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) in enhancing communication and collaboration in construction projects, specifically within the Philippine construction industry. By addressing persistent challenges such as miscommunication, information overload, lack of role clarity, and poor decision-making processes, the research seeks to provide actionable insights for construction professionals to optimize BIM implementation. These optimizations aim to reduce misinterpretations, streamline workflows, enhance decision-making, and improve overall project outcomes, addressing critical issues in the construction process. The research underscores the importance of adopting innovative technologies like BIM to transform communication and collaboration processes, enhancing project efficiency, resilience, and quality. It aligns with Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 9: Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure by promoting technological innovation and improving the efficiency and sustainability of construction projects. Additionally, it supports SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production by demonstrating how structured information management through BIM can reduce errors, minimize resource wastage, and promote lifecycle efficiency in construction practices.
The findings of this study will benefit a wide range of stakeholders within the construction industry. For construction professionals, the research offers strategies to overcome communication barriers, optimize processes, and leverage BIM's potential in managing complex projects. Policymakers can utilize these insights to develop guidelines that encourage the adoption of BIM and promote sustainable practices across the sector. Researchers will find valuable information to advance studies on BIM applications for addressing communication and collaboration challenges. Furthermore, clients and project owners will benefit from enhanced project transparency, improved stakeholder engagement, and increased trust in project delivery. By addressing critical communication issues and showcasing BIM’s potential, this research contributes to the advancement of the Philippine construction industry, fostering innovation, sustainability, and operational efficiency in the sector.

C.1 General Objective

This study aimed to assess the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool within the Philippine construction industry. It focused on how BIM addressed common project challenges such as miscommunication, information gaps, unclear responsibilities, and delays in decision-making. The study also provided insights into current practices and briefly considered how these practices aligned with internationally recognized frameworks such as ISO 19650, where relevant.
C.2 Specific Objectives
1. To assess the effectiveness of BIM in enhancing communication and collaboration within the Philippine construction industry by examining five parameters: (a) Project Efficiency, (b) Stakeholder Engagement, (c) Decision-Making Processes, (d) Cost and Resource Optimization, and (e) Risk Mitigation and Quality Improvement.
2. To identify the key challenges and barriers faced in implementing BIM as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry, focusing on how these obstacles affect the five parameters of BIM effectiveness identified in specific objective number 1.
3. To determine the management practices and strategies that can address these challenges and barriers, ensuring alignment with the five parameters of BIM effectiveness identified in specific objective number 1, and compare these practices with ISO 19650 standards for information management.
4. To determine the significant difference in perception among key stakeholders when they are grouped according to (a) contractors, (b) project managers, (c) supervisors which include architects/engineers, and (d) clients with regard to the effectiveness, challenges, and potential of BIM as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry, with reference to the five parameters of BIM effectiveness identified in specific objective number 1.
5. To investigate the significant relationships between demographic factors (e.g., role, years of experience, type of organization) and the five parameters of BIM effectiveness (i.e., Project Efficiency, Stakeholder Engagement, Decision-Making, Cost and Resource Optimization, and Risk Mitigation & Quality Improvement).

D. Scope and Delimitation
This study focused on assessing the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool within the Philippine construction industry. It examined how BIM addressed persistent challenges such as miscommunication, delayed information exchange, unclear task responsibilities, and inconsistent stakeholder coordination.​
The research encompassed a diverse range of construction projects, including residential, commercial, infrastructure, and industrial developments. It centered on five key performance parameters: (1) Project Efficiency, (2) Stakeholder Engagement, (3) Decision-Making Processes, (4) Cost and Resource Optimization, and (5) Risk Mitigation and Quality Improvement.​
Participants included construction professionals with practical experience in using BIM tools for communication and coordination. These comprised project managers, engineers, contractors, architects, and clients.
While the study primarily focused on BIM as the main platform for communication and collaboration, it briefly considered the alignment of observed practices with selected principles from ISO 19650, particularly concerning structured communication and data management. Tools and platforms not integrated with BIM, such as informal messaging apps and unstructured email threads, were excluded from the core analysis.​
The findings were therefore most applicable to construction teams and organizations engaged in BIM-enabled projects, offering insights into how BIM supported collaboration across various project types and stakeholder roles within the Philippine context.​
