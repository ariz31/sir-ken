METHODOLOGY
A. Research Design
This study adopted a descriptive-correlational research design to systematically evaluate the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool within the Philippine construction industry. Through this design, the study aimed to quantify the relationships among BIM adoption levels, stakeholder involvement, and communication effectiveness, and to identify significant differences in perceptions across distinct stakeholder groups. The dependent variable, communication effectiveness, was assessed based on five metrics: timeliness, accuracy, clarity, efficiency, and stakeholder engagement. Independent variables included the level of BIM adoption (basic, intermediate, advanced) and stakeholder category (contractor, project manager, architect/engineer, and client), while critical mediating factors such as role clarity, decision-making responsibilities, information management rigor, and timeliness of updates were considered to fully capture BIM's multidimensional influence on project outcomes.
Data collection involved the administration of structured questionnaires distributed to targeted stakeholders, including architects, engineers, contractors, project managers, and clients who actively participated in BIM-enabled construction projects. These questionnaires measured stakeholder perceptions of BIM’s effectiveness in facilitating communication, enhancing collaboration, and improving decision-making processes, while also identifying practical challenges and potential strategies for effective BIM implementation. Following data collection, responses were systematically prepared, cleaned, and reviewed to ensure accuracy and reliability. Descriptive statistical methods, such as mean, standard deviation, and frequency distributions, were then used to summarize data trends and patterns.
Inferential statistical techniques further analyzed the data to understand deeper relationships and differences among groups. Specifically, Pearson correlation was employed to examine the strength and direction of associations between BIM adoption, stakeholder involvement, and communication effectiveness metrics. Additionally, Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was utilized to compare communication effectiveness across different BIM adoption levels and stakeholder groups, supplemented by post-hoc tests to identify specific group differences. The reliability of the survey instruments was ensured through Cronbach’s Alpha, while statistical assumptions, such as normality and homogeneity of variances, were thoroughly validated.
Results derived from the data analysis were presented through clear and concise visualizations, including tables, charts, and graphs, to effectively highlight significant findings. Ultimately, this descriptive-correlational research design enabled the study to provide comprehensive, actionable insights into how BIM influences communication and collaboration, identify barriers limiting its effective implementation, and recommend strategies for maximizing its potential within the Philippine construction sector.

C.   Population Sampling
This study utilized a purposive sampling method to systematically select participants who possessed relevant expertise and hands-on experience with Building Information Modeling (BIM) in construction projects across the Philippines. This targeted sampling approach was chosen to ensure that the collected data provided meaningful and detailed insights regarding BIM’s effectiveness as a communication and collaboration tool. The participant pool included key stakeholders actively engaged in BIM-based projects, specifically architects, engineers, contractors, project managers, and clients, who were identified based on their direct involvement and substantial experience with BIM processes and tools.
According to the Construction Industry Authority of the Philippines (CIAP), there were 16,300 active Philippine Contractors Accreditation Board (PCAB)-licensed contractors as of August 2023. Based on the findings of a national survey by Rodriguez, Bagcal, Baccay, and Barbier (2019), approximately 33.6% of professionals in the Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) industry actively employed BIM. Consequently, the estimated population size for this study was approximately 5,477 professionals (33.6% of 16,300 licensed contractors) actively using BIM.

D.   Research Instrument
This study employs a mixed-methods approach to assess the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool in construction projects. The primary data collection tool is a structured survey questionnaire complemented by semi-structured interviews. These instruments are designed to gather both quantitative and qualitative data, providing a comprehensive understanding of BIM’s role in improving communication, fostering collaboration, and addressing challenges in the Philippine construction industry. The questionnaire targets stakeholders actively involved in BIM-based projects, such as project managers, engineers, architects, contractors, subcontractors, and clients/owners.
The questionnaire is divided into six sections, each aligned with the study’s objectives. The demographics section gathers essential background information, including participants’ roles, years of experience, familiarity with BIM, and types of projects they have worked on. This section ensures respondent diversity and provides context for analyzing responses. The BIM adoption section evaluates BIM’s influence on communication metrics such as timeliness, accuracy, and clarity. Using a Likert scale (ranging from 1, "Strongly Disagree," to 5, "Strongly Agree"), this section quantifies the extent to which BIM enhances communication and collaboration.
The stakeholder collaboration and engagement section explores how BIM fosters teamwork, clarifies roles, and enhances stakeholder participation. This section uses a mix of closed-ended and open-ended questions to measure how well BIM aligns stakeholder objectives and improves collaboration. The communication effectiveness section focuses on key metrics—timeliness, clarity, accuracy, efficiency, and sustainability—assessing how effectively BIM facilitates communication and reduces rework due to miscommunication or errors.
The challenges and recommendations section identifies obstacles in BIM implementation and gathers actionable suggestions for improvement. Closed-ended questions help quantify common challenges, while open-ended questions provide participants an opportunity to elaborate on specific issues and propose practical solutions. Semi-structured interviews complement the questionnaire by offering deeper insights into participants' experiences, perspectives, and challenges. Interviews provide qualitative context to the quantitative data, ensuring a holistic analysis of BIM’s effectiveness.
The questionnaire was designed based on key themes from existing literature on BIM and communication effectiveness, focusing on role clarity, decision-making responsibilities, information management, timeliness of updates, and stakeholder engagement. The closed-ended questions ensure measurable and comparable responses, while open-ended questions allow for nuanced and detailed input. Standard deviation will be used to assess the variability or consistency in survey responses, particularly in evaluating key metrics such as communication timeliness, accuracy, and clarity. This statistical measure will provide insights into how closely the responses are clustered around the mean, helping to interpret the degree of agreement or diversity in stakeholder perceptions of BIM’s effectiveness. For data analysis, Pearson Correlation will be used to measure relationships between variables such as BIM adoption and communication effectiveness, while ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) will compare differences across BIM adoption levels and stakeholder groups.
This research instrument ensures alignment with the study's objectives, capturing diverse perspectives and providing actionable insights into the effectiveness of BIM in improving communication and collaboration in construction projects. By integrating structured quantitative metrics and qualitative feedback, this approach enables a comprehensive evaluation of BIM’s contributions to the Philippine construction industry.

E.	Data Gathering Procedure
This study employed a mixed-methods approach to comprehensively assess the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool in the Philippine construction industry. The primary data collection instruments consisted of a structured survey questionnaire, complemented by semi-structured interviews. Together, these tools were designed to capture both quantitative and qualitative data, allowing for a well-rounded understanding of how BIM contributes to enhancing communication, improving collaboration, and addressing key implementation challenges in real-world construction environments. The instruments were administered to a targeted group of stakeholders actively involved in BIM-based projects, including architects, engineers, project managers, contractors, subcontractors, and clients.
The survey questionnaire was divided into six major sections, each aligned with the study’s objectives. The demographic section collected essential background information such as the respondent’s professional role, years of experience, type of organization, number of BIM projects handled, and level of BIM familiarity. This data provided context for interpreting patterns and differences among stakeholder groups. The BIM adoption section focused on evaluating the extent to which respondents' organizations had adopted BIM and how BIM influenced key communication metrics such as timeliness, accuracy, and clarity. Responses in this section were measured using a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (“Strongly Disagree”) to 5 (“Strongly Agree”) to quantify perceptions in a structured and analyzable format.
The stakeholder collaboration and engagement section explored how BIM influenced teamwork, stakeholder coordination, and role clarity. This section incorporated both closed-ended questions to allow for statistical comparison and open-ended items to capture nuanced insights on how BIM aligned objectives across disciplines. The communication effectiveness section addressed metrics such as the frequency and clarity of updates, accuracy of shared information, and efficiency of coordination workflows. It also included questions to assess the extent to which BIM reduced rework and miscommunication.
The challenges and recommendations section identified common obstacles encountered during BIM implementation, such as technical, organizational, and cultural barriers. Closed-ended questions helped quantify the prevalence of these challenges, while open-ended questions encouraged respondents to provide specific recommendations based on their experience. In parallel, semi-structured interviews were conducted with a subset of survey respondents to gain deeper, narrative insights into their real-life experiences, decision-making processes, and views on how BIM affected their collaborative efforts and project outcomes. These interviews provided qualitative depth and enriched the interpretation of the survey findings.
The development of the questionnaire was informed by existing literature on BIM and communication effectiveness, focusing on recurring themes such as role clarity, timely updates, structured data management, and stakeholder engagement. Closed-ended items allowed for the calculation of descriptive and inferential statistics, while open-ended responses contributed qualitative evidence. Standard deviation was applied to assess the variability in responses across stakeholder groups and adoption levels, especially regarding key communication indicators such as timeliness, clarity, and accuracy.
For data analysis, Pearson Correlation was used to examine the relationships between variables such as BIM adoption level and communication effectiveness, while Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was used to detect statistically significant differences in perceptions across stakeholder roles and BIM maturity levels. Together, these analytical tools supported the study in deriving actionable conclusions and recommendations.
In sum, the research instrument was thoughtfully designed to align with the study’s objectives and to ensure the reliability and validity of data collected. By integrating quantitative metrics with qualitative feedback, the study delivered a holistic evaluation of BIM’s communicative and collaborative impact, contributing valuable insights for improving project coordination and management in the Philippine construction industry.

F.	Data Analysis Procedure
The data analysis procedure for this study followed a systematic and structured approach to evaluate the effectiveness of Building Information Modeling (BIM) as a communication and collaboration tool in construction projects across the Philippines. The process began with data preparation and cleaning, which involved reviewing the dataset for completeness, accuracy, and consistency. Incomplete or inconsistent responses were addressed through data validation techniques, and any outliers or anomalies were carefully examined and handled to maintain the integrity and reliability of the data.
Descriptive statistics were computed to provide a comprehensive summary of the dataset. Measures such as mean, standard deviation, and frequency distribution were used to identify trends and patterns across various stakeholder groups and BIM adoption levels. These descriptive insights offered a foundational understanding of how different respondents perceived the impact of BIM on communication and collaboration.
To ensure the reliability of the survey instrument, Cronbach’s Alpha was calculated. A value of 0.70 or higher was considered indicative of acceptable internal consistency, thereby validating the coherence of the questionnaire items in measuring the intended variables.
The primary inferential statistical methods employed in this study included Pearson Correlation and Analysis of Variance (ANOVA). Pearson Correlation was used to assess the strength and direction of the linear relationship between independent variables—such as BIM adoption levels, stakeholder categories, and mediating factors (e.g., role clarity, information management, timeliness of updates, and technology readiness)—and the dependent variable, communication effectiveness. ANOVA was applied to examine whether significant differences existed in communication effectiveness across groups based on varying BIM adoption levels and stakeholder roles. Post-hoc tests, such as Tukey's HSD, were conducted following significant ANOVA results to identify specific group differences and gain deeper insights into the variation in stakeholder perceptions.
The statistical assumptions underlying Pearson Correlation and ANOVA—such as normality of data distribution and homogeneity of variances—were rigorously tested. Where necessary, data transformations or alternative non-parametric statistical methods were considered to ensure the validity of the analyses. A p-value threshold of 0.05 was used to determine statistical significance in all inferential tests.
Qualitative data collected from open-ended questionnaire responses and semi-structured interviews were analyzed using thematic analysis. This involved identifying recurring themes, patterns, and insights related to the use of BIM for communication and collaboration. The qualitative findings served to enrich the quantitative results by providing contextual depth and uncovering nuanced stakeholder experiences and perspectives.
All findings were synthesized and presented using clear visualizations such as tables, charts, and graphs. These visual tools were designed to facilitate interpretation and highlight key relationships, differences, and trends across stakeholder groups and BIM maturity levels. Ultimately, this data analysis procedure ensured a rigorous and comprehensive evaluation of how BIM adoption, stakeholder engagement, and ISO 19650-aligned practices influenced communication and collaboration effectiveness in the Philippine construction industry. The insights gained contributed to actionable recommendations for optimizing BIM processes and enhancing collaborative project delivery.
